About me

Hi, I'm Steve. A Full Stack Software Engineer

I have been writing code for over 10 years. I Started with HTML,CSS and JavaScript; then I moved on to creating dynamic pages with PHP. I taught myself PHP by following tutorials and reading other developer's code, In my early years I was a programmer. However, once I discovered the concept of software engineering, I quickly adopted better ways of developing software and vowed to evolve from a programmer to an engineer.

During my time studying in university, I started a small business(LLC) developing websites and applications for local companies in Southern California. I learned a lot about interfacing with customers, contract writing and agile development during this time. I interned with a few organizations until finally settling at California Steel Industries.

While working for California Steel Industries, I continued to learn and absorb everything. I created Human Machine Interfaces, cleaned up their code base, relieving the organization of much of their technical debt, and converted programs from C, C++, and VB to C#. I was offered a full time position before graduating from college and accepted. I quickly became their top developer, mentoring not only junior and intern process control engineers, but also giving design advice to the senior engineers.

I've spent most of my time working on web applications. I enjoy working with web applications because often times the developer gets to work on all aspects of the project. From the front-end to the back-end and also the database. I consider myself a full stack developer. I also enjoy going from conception to implementation very rapidly. I like to stub out ideas and create prototypes. Making progress on problems greatly increases my motivation and enthusiasm. However, although my experience in web application development is most prevalent; I genuinely just enjoy software development in general.

My approach to software development is pretty simple. Write code in as simple of a manner as possible, but not any simpler. While in college I would have family members read my code (they were not techies like me) and ask them if it was clear what my program or software was doing. I took pride in the fact that lay people were able to understand my source code. I didn't expect them to understand some of the algorithms that I was using, but I commented on parts that were not easily identifiable, created semantic names for variable, functions and classes, and logically structured code in groups. These things seem fundamental, but I am surprised by how often I don't see this approach used in practice. In essence I try to follow a few core principles:

  1. Do it right the first time.
  2. Make it easy to follow!
  3. Be pragmatic.
  4. Have Fun!
